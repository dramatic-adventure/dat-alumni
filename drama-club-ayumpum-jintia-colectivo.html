No. III · The Room

What happens when the drums begin.

Rehearsal opens with elders' words and warm-ups in Shuar. By the second hour, the room belongs to the youth — devising scenes that braid ancestral story with the politics of staying. A character can be a river, a grandmother, a logging truck, a joke about a cousin.

The collective's plays travel to neighboring communities by truck and on foot, performed in plazas and schoolyards, in the language the audience speaks at home.

What visiting artists bring

New eyes on the forest — devising techniques, vocal craft, dramaturgy, and a way of looking that re-frames what the youth already know.

What the club shares back

Shuar language, songs, and the responsibilities of being guardians of this land — passed across the rehearsal circle without translation when none is needed.

No. VI · Local Context

The forest is contested. The room is not.

This club sits in Amazonian territory where outside pressures and economic instability are part of daily life. The club offers a steady, joyful space rooted in culture and collective resistance — somewhere the young Shuar can rehearse not only plays but the future of a place.

i. Shared Need Consistent transportation for youth from neighboring communities.
ii. Shared Need Stipends for youth facilitators and translators.
iii. Shared Need Funds to document and archive Shuar-language plays and songs.
